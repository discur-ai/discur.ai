## Anthropic

**URL:** anthropic.com
**Hero:** "AI research and products that put safety at the frontier"
**Sub:** "AI will have a vast impact on the world. Anthropic is a public benefit corporation dedicated to securing its benefits and mitigating its risks."

**Section structure (6 sections):**
1. Hero with interactive globe visualization (~40 words)
2. Latest releases -- product cards (Claude Opus 4.6 etc.)
3. Featured content blocks (research, initiatives, education)
4. Research team descriptions
5. Publications list
6. Footer navigation

**Word density:** Hero section is extremely sparse -- 40 words total. The globe visualization does the heavy lifting. Supporting sections use ~50-80 word blocks.

**Voice:** First-person plural ("we build," "we're using") for institutional statements. Third-person for product descriptions. Active voice dominates. Measured, never breathless. No exclamation marks.

**Specificity:** Selective and deliberate. Numbers appear only where they matter:
- "81,000 people" (in a study)
- "3,000 hours of red teaming"
- Model tier names (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku) without benchmark numbers on the homepage

They do NOT cite benchmarks on the landing page. Performance claims live in model cards and technical reports, never the homepage.

**Credibility for researchers:** HIGH. The restraint is the signal. No superlatives, no "revolutionary." The research page layers from accessible mission statement -> specific outcomes -> full papers. A PhD ML researcher would find this credible because it reads like a research organization's page, not a marketing page.

**Best pattern to steal:** The gradient approach to depth. Hero is plain language. One click deeper gets you specific numbers. Another click gets you the paper. This mirrors Shneiderman's mantra: "Overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand." Anthropic's research page does this perfectly:
- Layer 1: "investigate the safety, inner workings, and societal impacts of AI models"
- Layer 2: "Constitutional Classifiers filter the overwhelming majority of jailbreaks"
- Layer 3: Full technical paper

**Worst pattern to avoid:** The globe visualization is somewhat self-indulgent -- it's a lot of engineering for marginal communication value. For Discur, avoid expensive interactive elements that don't teach the reader something about the technology.

## Google DeepMind

**URL:** deepmind.google
**Hero:** "Explore our next generation AI systems"
**Sub:** (none -- drops directly into product cards)

**Research page hero:** "We work on some of the most complex and interesting challenges in AI"
**About page mission:** "Our mission is to build AI responsibly to benefit humanity"

**Section structure (homepage, 4 sections):**
1. Hero + product card carousel (Gemini, Veo, Gemini Robotics)
2. Research breakthroughs carousel (8 projects)
3. Latest news (6 posts)
4. Publications (6 papers)

**Word density:** Extremely sparse per section. Hero is 7 words. Product cards are 5-10 words each. The page is almost entirely visual with project card thumbnails.

**Voice:** Third-person institutional ("Our mission is..."). Aspirational but measured. Passive/active blend. Uses "we" for the organization, "our" for possessive framing. Never addresses the reader directly.

**Specificity:** Almost none on the homepage. No benchmarks, no metrics, no numbers. Just project names (AlphaFold, AlphaGo, WeatherNext) which carry their own weight because these are well-known results. The only temporal specificity: "10 years of AlphaGo."

This works because DeepMind has brand equity -- the project names ARE the proof. A startup cannot do this.

**Credibility for researchers:** MEDIUM. The homepage reads as a portfolio site, not a research org. Researchers would go straight to the publications page. The marketing layer feels corporate-Google: safe, broad, uninspiring. But the underlying research speaks for itself.

**Best pattern to steal:** Project names as proof. "AlphaFold" in a heading carries more weight than any benchmark number. If Discur can name the octonionic trie and make it stick, the name itself becomes a credibility marker. Name things boldly and specifically.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** The vague aspirational hero. "Explore our next generation AI systems" says nothing. It could be any AI company. "Most complex and interesting challenges" is throat-clearing. Avoid this kind of corporate emptiness.

## OpenAI

**URL:** openai.com/research/
**Hero:** "We believe our research will eventually lead to artificial general intelligence, a system that can solve human-level problems."
**Sub:** "Building safe and beneficial AGI is our mission."

**Section structure:** Research page organizes as:
1. Mission statement hero
2. Research index with filtering (Release, Publication, Conclusion categories)
3. Chronological publication list
4. Stories/narratives section

**Word density:** The hero is 22 words -- one sentence. Dense and declarative. The "ten years" retrospective by Sam Altman is long-form (1000+ words) but this is a blog post, not landing copy.

**Voice:** First-person plural throughout. "We believe," "our research," "our mission." Active voice. Confident, bordering on grandiose. The key rhetorical move: stating belief as organizational fact. "We believe our research will eventually lead to AGI" -- this is not hedged. It's a declaration of intent.

**Specificity:** On the research page itself, almost none. The specificity lives in the individual papers. The framing layer is pure narrative: belief, mission, intent.

From the "ten years" post: "In ten more years, I believe we are almost certain to build superintelligence." No benchmarks, no timelines with error bars -- just conviction.

**Credibility for researchers:** LOW-MEDIUM. The AGI framing is polarizing. Many ML researchers find the "we're building AGI" positioning either naive or manipulative. The individual papers are excellent. But the marketing wrapper turns off skeptics. The gap between the landing copy's grandiosity and the papers' rigor creates dissonance.

**Best pattern to steal:** The single-sentence mission hero. 22 words, one claim, total clarity. You know exactly what OpenAI believes and is building. For Discur: one sentence stating what the octonionic trie IS and what it DOES. No preamble.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** Grandiose claims without inline evidence. "Will eventually lead to artificial general intelligence" is unfalsifiable and unverifiable. Researchers smell this. If Discur claims "self-organizing memory," it must show the mechanism on the same page, not ask you to trust the claim.

## Cohere

**URL:** cohere.com
**Hero:** "Your next breakthrough, powered by AI"
**Sub:** "Cohere is where powerful AI meets practical business solutions -- so you can work smarter."

**Section structure (9 sections):**
1. Hero with dual CTA ("Request a demo" / "Explore products")
2. Trust logos (20+ enterprise clients)
3. Three-column features ("Safe. Flexible. Built for business.")
4. Product spotlight (North platform)
5. Industry solutions (7 vertical cards)
6. Model showcase (Command, Transcribe, Embed, Rerank)
7. Developer resources
8. Social proof quote (Fujitsu)
9. Newsletter signup

**Word density:** Medium. Each section block is 40-80 words. The three-column section uses single-word headings ("Security," "Deployment," "Customization") with 20-word descriptions.

**Voice:** Third-person, business-focused. "Cohere is where..." Second-person for value props ("your breakthrough," "you can"). Active voice. Enterprise sales tone -- benefits over mechanisms.

**Specificity:** Low-medium. "Supports 23 languages" is specific. "Highly accurate text outputs" is not. Model names (Command, Embed, Rerank) are specific but the descriptions are benefit-level, not mechanism-level. No benchmarks, no comparisons, no code samples on the landing page.

**Credibility for researchers:** LOW. This reads as enterprise SaaS marketing. "Your next breakthrough, powered by AI" could be any company. The copy optimizes for procurement officers, not researchers. A PhD ML researcher would find nothing here to evaluate technically.

**Best pattern to steal:** Nothing -- this is the anti-model for Discur. The enterprise SaaS playbook (logo walls, vertical cards, "request a demo") is exactly what a research-focused deep tech company should NOT do.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** "Your next breakthrough, powered by AI" -- this is the canonical example of content-free hero copy. It makes no specific claim, reveals nothing about the technology, and could be pasted onto any AI company's site without modification. If you can swap your company name with any competitor's and the copy still works, the copy has failed.

## Mistral AI

**URL:** mistral.ai
**Hero:** "Frontier AI. In your hands."
**Sub:** "We help organizations build tailored AI systems to solve the world's hardest problems."

**Section structure (9 sections):**
1. Hero with dual CTA ("Get in touch" / "Start building")
2. Customer logos carousel
3. Four core value props ("Your AI future belongs in your hands" / "Frontier intelligence, customized to you" / "Enterprise agents with deep context" / "Self-contained private deployments")
4. Three customer case studies (Stellantis, ASML, CMA CGM)
5. Six product/service offerings with bullet features
6. Platform architecture visual
7. Deployment options (privacy messaging)
8. Hiring CTA
9. Footer

**Word density:** Hero is 5 words (headline) + 15 words (sub). Tight. Value prop headings are 4-7 words each. Each feature block has 3-5 bullet points of ~10 words.

**Voice:** Second-person direct address ("your hands," "your AI," "customized to you"). Active voice. Positioning as enabler, not provider. The possessive framing ("your AI future," "your hands") is deliberate -- sovereignty/control messaging for European positioning.

**Specificity:** Zero benchmarks. Zero metrics. Zero technical details on the landing page. The "frontier" claim is unsubstantiated by any number. Capability bullets are generic: "workflow automation, enterprise search, content creation." Product names exist (Le Chat, Codestral) but with no technical depth.

**Credibility for researchers:** LOW-MEDIUM. The 5-word hero ("Frontier AI. In your hands.") is memorable and has swagger. But nothing backs it up. A researcher would need to navigate to the model cards or papers to find anything evaluable. The landing page is a sales page, not a research page.

**Best pattern to steal:** The 5-word hero. "Frontier AI. In your hands." -- this is muscular economy. Sentence fragments. Period after each. The rhythm works. For Discur, something equally terse that names the actual thing: "The octonionic trie." or "Memory that organizes itself."

**Worst pattern to avoid:** "Solve the world's hardest problems" is emptier than it sounds. Which problems? How? This is the enterprise AI equivalent of "we're passionate about making the world a better place." Never claim to solve unspecified problems.

## Hugging Face

**URL:** huggingface.co
**Hero:** "The AI community building the future."
**Sub:** "The platform where the machine learning community collaborates on models, datasets, and applications."

**Section structure (7 sections):**
1. Hero with CTA ("Browse 2M+ models")
2. Trending section (Models / Spaces / Datasets -- 5 each)
3. Value propositions ("The Home of Machine Learning" with 4 pillars)
4. Paid offerings (Team & Enterprise, Inference, Compute)
5. Social proof ("More than 50,000 organizations" + 8 logos)
6. Open source libraries (11 cards with GitHub star counts)
7. Footer

**Word density:** Hero is 19 words. Moderate throughout. The trending section is almost entirely project names and star counts -- the content IS the community activity.

**Voice:** Third-person, community-focused. "The platform where..." Not "we built" but "the community collaborates." Inclusive, non-hierarchical. Action verbs: "Host," "collaborate," "deploy." The voice positions HF as infrastructure, not agent.

**Specificity:** VERY HIGH -- the best of any company analyzed. Specific numbers everywhere:
- "2M+ models"
- "1M+ applications"
- "500k+ datasets"
- "45,000+ models via Inference Providers"
- "50,000+ organizations"
- "$20/user/month"
- "$0.60/hour GPU compute"
- GitHub stars per library (e.g., Transformers: 158,606)

No benchmarks or performance claims -- the numbers are scale/adoption metrics. The proof is the ecosystem size, not a single model's performance.

**Credibility for researchers:** HIGH. Researchers use Hugging Face daily. The page doesn't need to persuade -- it reflects a reality researchers already know. The GitHub star counts and model counts are verifiable. The trending section shows real-time community activity. Nothing here needs to be taken on faith.

**Best pattern to steal:** Verifiable numbers as proof. Every number on HF's page can be independently verified. Star counts link to repos. Model counts link to search results. Discur should adopt this: if you cite a number, link to the evidence. If you can't link to evidence, don't cite the number.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** "Building the future" is a weak hero for a company this strong. The actual content (2M models, trending repos, star counts) does all the work. The hero contributes nothing. Lesson: if your content is strong, don't weaken it with generic framing.

## Wolfram Mathematica

**URL:** wolfram.com/mathematica/
**Hero:** "Wolfram Mathematica: The world's definitive system for modern technical computing"
**Sub:** "Available on desktop, cloud & mobile"

**Section structure (6 sections):**
1. Hero / Introduction (~100 words)
2. "For Modern Technical Computing, There's No Other Choice" (~500 words, 9 subsections)
3. Coverage (~150 words, domain list)
4. Highlighted Core Areas (~50 words, 13 domains listed)
5. Core Technologies (~300 words, 6 components)
6. The Mathematica Trajectory (~350 words, historical)

**Total estimated word count:** ~1,450 words. Substantially more text-heavy than any AI company page analyzed.

**Word density per section:** The "No Other Choice" section is the heaviest at ~500 words, broken into 9 thematic subsections with headings like:
- "A Vast System, All Integrated"
- "Not Just Numbers, Not Just Math -- But Everything"
- "Unimaginable Algorithm Power"
- "Industrial-strength capabilities"

**Voice:** Third-person declarative. Never "we." The product is the subject: "Mathematica has defined the state of the art." This is important -- Wolfram treats Mathematica as an entity that EXISTS and HAS PROPERTIES, not something a team built. It's closer to describing a natural phenomenon than a product. Active voice throughout but with the product as agent.

**Specificity:** VERY HIGH -- the highest of any page analyzed:
- "over 6,000 built-in functions"
- "500+ functions from Mathematica 1"
- "250+ file formats"
- "150,000+ examples"
- "13,000 open-code Demonstrations"
- "three decades" of development
- Current version: "Mathematica 14.3"

Every claim is backed by a number. No hedging. Statements presented as established facts, not claims.

**Credibility for researchers:** HIGH. This is the benchmark for technical product marketing aimed at scientists. Wolfram has 30+ years of credibility. The page works because:
1. It names specific capabilities with numbers
2. It claims a category ("definitive system") and backs it with scope
3. It acknowledges its own history (the Trajectory section)
4. The density of information signals seriousness

A mathematician reading this page can evaluate whether Mathematica solves their problems. The page provides enough specificity to be useful, not just persuasive.

**Best pattern to steal:** THREE patterns:

1. **Product-as-subject voice.** "Mathematica defines the state of the art" not "we built a great product." Discur should say "The octonionic trie organizes memory in eight dimensions" not "we built a system that organizes memory."

2. **Quantified claims with no hedging.** "6,000 built-in functions" not "thousands of functions." "250+ file formats" not "supports many formats." If Discur has a number, use the exact number.

3. **Category-defining headline.** "The world's definitive system for modern technical computing" -- this claims a category and fills it. Discur could try: "The first algebraic data structure for self-organizing memory" (if true).

**Worst pattern to avoid:** The "Unimaginable Algorithm Power" heading is the one weak spot. "Unimaginable" is the kind of intensifier that Zachary Lipton's writing heuristics warn against. It's subjective and unverifiable. Every other heading on the Wolfram page is specific; this one is pure marketing.

## Stripe

**URL:** stripe.com
**Hero:** "Financial infrastructure to grow your revenue."
**Sub:** "Accept payments, offer financial services, and implement custom revenue models -- from your first transaction to your billionth."

**Section structure (8 sections):**
1. Hero with dual CTA ("Get started" / "Sign up with Google")
2. Customer logos (Amazon, Shopify, Figma, Uber)
3. Solutions section ("Flexible solutions for every business model")
4. Statistics block ("The backbone of global commerce")
5. Business segments (Enterprises, Startups, Platforms)
6. Infrastructure/Developer section
7. News carousel
8. Footer

**Word density:** Hero is 8 words (headline) + 18 words (sub). Total 26 words to communicate the entire value proposition. The statistics block uses numbers-as-content, not prose.

**Voice:** Third-person, outcome-focused. "Financial infrastructure to grow YOUR revenue" -- second-person possessive. Active voice. Imperative mood in sub ("Accept... offer... implement..."). The three verbs are a deliberate progression from simple to complex.

**Specificity:** EXCEPTIONAL. The statistics block is the strongest proof section of any company analyzed:
- "135+ currencies and payment methods supported"
- "$1.9T in payments volume processed in 2025"
- "99.999% historical uptime"
- "200M+ active subscriptions"
- "500M+ API requests per day"
- "150K+ transactions per minute"

Six metrics. Each one verifiable. Each one a different axis (breadth, scale, reliability, frequency). This is proof by overwhelming evidence.

**Credibility for researchers:** N/A (developer audience, not research). But the PATTERN is highly credible: specific, verifiable, multi-axis. A developer reading this page can evaluate whether Stripe handles their scale. The page provides decision-making information, not just brand sentiment.

**Best pattern to steal:** The multi-axis proof block. Stripe doesn't just say "we process a lot of payments." They give you six different lenses on scale. Discur should consider: what are 4-6 different measurable properties of the octonionic trie? Dimensionality (8), associator structure, retrieval complexity, self-organization speed? Present them as a proof block, not buried in prose.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** Nothing significant. Stripe's copy is nearly flawless for its audience. The only caution for Discur: Stripe is selling a developer tool with measurable outcomes. Discur is selling a research concept. Don't copy Stripe's transactional framing ("grow your revenue") -- adapt the proof-block pattern to research claims.

**Docs page note:** stripe.com/docs (now docs.stripe.com) uses the imperative mood throughout: "Explore," "Sell," "Set up." Action-oriented with minimal descriptive text. Product names stand alone without explanation, assuming developer familiarity. The navigation structure itself communicates hierarchy. This is relevant for Discur if it ever has a docs section, but not for the landing page.

## Linear

**URL:** linear.app
**Hero:** "Linear is a purpose-built tool for planning and building products."
**Sub:** "Meet the system for modern software development. Streamline issues, projects, and product roadmaps."

**Section structure:** Primarily visual-first, CSS-animation-heavy. The page is mostly interactive demonstrations with minimal text blocks. Linear relies on product screenshots and animations to make its case, not copy.

**Word density:** Hero is 11 words. Sub is 15 words. Total hero section: 26 words. Sections throughout are similarly sparse -- Linear lets the product interface speak.

**Voice:** Third-person declarative for the brand statement ("Linear is..."). Second-person for the call to engagement ("Meet the system..."). The word "purpose-built" does a lot of work -- it implies intentionality and specificity without explaining the mechanism.

**Specificity:** LOW on the landing page. No benchmarks, no comparisons, no metrics. Social proof is implicit ("20,000+ companies including OpenAI, Ramp, Vercel") rather than quantified performance. The specificity comes from the product itself -- the UI animations show exactly what Linear does.

**Credibility for researchers:** N/A (product tool, not research). But the pattern is instructive: "purpose-built" is a credibility signal. It says "we designed this for exactly your problem, not a general tool adapted for your use case."

**Best pattern to steal:** "Purpose-built." This single compound word communicates more than a paragraph of explanation. It implies: (1) we know exactly what problem we're solving, (2) we chose to solve only this problem, (3) the tool was designed from first principles for this use case. For Discur, the analogous move is to signal that the octonionic trie wasn't adapted from something else -- it was designed from algebraic first principles specifically for self-organizing memory.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** The visual-first approach only works if you HAVE a product to show. Discur is a research company without a shipped product UI. Copying Linear's "let the product speak" approach would leave Discur with empty space. The math and the trie structure must speak instead.

## Vercel

**URL:** vercel.com
**Hero:** "Build and deploy on the AI Cloud."
**Sub:** "Vercel provides the developer tools and cloud infrastructure to build, scale, and secure a faster, more personalized web."

**Section structure (13 sections):**
1. Hero with dual CTA
2. Customer metrics (3 customer stories with numbers)
3. Use case cards (AI Apps, Web Apps, Ecommerce, Marketing, Platforms)
4. "Your product, delivered"
5. Agents
6. AI Apps
7. Web Apps
8. Composable Commerce
9. Multi-tenant Platform
10. Framework-Defined Infrastructure
11. Scale/security messaging
12. Product spotlights (Fluid Compute, AI Gateway)
13. Template deployment CTA

**Word density:** Hero is 8 words + 23 words sub. Most sections are 50-150 words. The page is LONG -- 13 sections is more than most competitors. But each section is individually sparse.

**Voice:** Second-person throughout ("your product," "your app," "your stack"). Active voice. Imperative CTAs ("Build," "Deploy," "Scale"). Developer-facing, assumes technical context. Coined terms used confidently: "The AI Cloud," "Fluid Compute," "Framework-Defined Infrastructure."

**Specificity:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Customer proof uses specific numbers:
- "build times went from 7m to 40s" (Runway)
- "95% reduction in page load times" (Leonardo.ai)
- "24x faster builds" (Zapier)
- "6x faster" (CI/CD claim)

But Vercel's OWN claims are benefit-level, not mechanism-level. "Globally performant" rather than "200ms p95 latency." The numbers are all attributed to customers, not to Vercel.

**Credibility for researchers:** N/A (developer infrastructure). But the PATTERN of citing customer metrics rather than self-reported metrics is relevant. Third-party proof > self-claim.

**Best pattern to steal:** Attributed proof. "Build times went from 7m to 40s" (Runway) is more credible than "40s build times" because the attribution makes it verifiable and the before/after shows magnitude. If Discur ever has users or benchmarks, cite them as "[result] -- [who achieved it]."

**Worst pattern to avoid:** 13 sections is too many. The page tries to be everything to everyone (AI apps, commerce, platforms, agents). This dilutes the message. Discur is a single-page landing for a single concept. Every section must serve the octonionic trie story.

## Basis AI

**URL:** basis.ai
**Hero:** "Creating a new kind of AI research organization."
**Sub (secondary headings, scroll-revealed):**
- "Advancing society's ability to solve hard problems."
- "Building a universal reasoning engine."

**About page hero:** "Basis is a nonprofit applied research organization with two mutually reinforcing goals."
**Mission:** (1) "understand and build intelligence" through mathematical principles and software implementation; (2) "advance society's ability to solve intractable problems."

**Section structure (homepage, 3 sections):**
1. Organization introduction (mission/identity)
2. Problem-solving focus (societal impact)
3. Technical foundation (reasoning engine)

Each section pairs with parallax scroll animations and full-width imagery. "Learn more" CTAs per section.

**About page structure:**
1. Mission statement
2. Who We Are (Team + Advisors, 30+ named members)
3. Contact

**Word density:** Extremely sparse on homepage -- three hero statements, each ~8 words, with scroll-based reveal. The about page is denser but still focused: mission + team credentials.

**Voice:** First-person plural ("we're building," "we can solve") mixed with third-person institutional ("Basis is a nonprofit..."). Active voice. Academic tone -- measured, unhurried.

**Specificity:** LOW on the homepage. "Universal reasoning engine" is conceptual. "Hard problems" is unspecified. "New kind of AI research organization" tells you what they ARE NOT more than what they ARE.

The about page compensates with credential-based specificity:
- "501(c)(3) nonprofit status"
- Specific institutional affiliations (MIT, Harvard, Columbia, Broad Institute)
- Named research areas (probabilistic programming, causal reasoning, neuroAI)
- Named output: "Pyro probabilistic programming language"

**Credibility for researchers:** MEDIUM-HIGH. The homepage alone is LOW (too vague). But the about page rescues it with real credentials and a named, verifiable output (Pyro). A researcher who clicks through to the about page would find people they may know or can look up. The team IS the proof.

**Best pattern to steal:** TWO patterns:

1. **Named, verifiable outputs.** Basis doesn't just say "we do probabilistic programming" -- they name "Pyro," which a researcher can go verify on GitHub (14k+ stars). Discur should name and link to anything verifiable: the octonionic trie implementation, the algebra, a paper.

2. **Credential stacking.** The about page lists team members with specific institutional affiliations and research areas. This is how small research orgs build credibility without products. Discur's team credentials should be prominent and specific.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** "Creating a new kind of AI research organization" -- this tells you nothing about the research. Every AI lab thinks it's a new kind of organization. The scroll-reveal homepage is aesthetically strong but informationally empty. Three hero statements, zero technical content. A researcher would leave the homepage having learned nothing about what Basis actually builds.

## Sakana AI

**URL:** sakana.ai
**Hero:** "We are an AI R&D company based in Tokyo, Japan."
**Sub:** "We want to develop AI solutions for Japan's needs, and democratize AI in Japan."

**Section structure:** Extremely minimal:
1. Logo
2. Two-sentence mission
3. CTA button
4. Navigation links (Chat, Blog, Careers, Company Info)

**Word density:** The entire homepage is approximately 30 words of content. This is the sparsest page in the analysis.

**Voice:** First-person plural, declarative. "We are," "We want." Simple present tense. Unpretentious. The sentence structure is almost ostentatiously simple -- subject-verb-object, no subordinate clauses.

**Specificity:** Almost none on the homepage. Geographic specificity (Tokyo, Japan) but zero technical specificity. The interesting work (AI Scientist, Evolutionary Model Merge, Continuous Thought Machines, Darwin Godel Machine) lives on the blog and company info pages, not the landing page.

**Credibility for researchers:** MEDIUM. The homepage earns credibility through restraint -- it doesn't overclaim. Researchers who recognize the founders (David Ha, Llion Jones -- co-inventor of the Transformer) already know what Sakana does. The homepage assumes you already know or will look further.

**Best pattern to steal:** Radical brevity. 30 words for an entire homepage is a bold choice. It says: "we don't need to convince you. The work speaks for itself." This only works if the work IS speaking somewhere (blog, papers, GitHub). For Discur, the lesson is: you CAN be brief if your technical content is strong elsewhere on the page. But Discur's octonionic trie is unknown -- it needs explanation that Sakana's known-quantity founders don't.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** "We want to develop AI solutions for Japan's needs" is surprisingly weak for a company with this pedigree. "We want to" signals aspiration, not achievement. "AI solutions" is generic. "Japan's needs" is vague. The gap between the team's actual research (Continuous Thought Machines, evolutionary model merging) and this pedestrian copy is jarring.

## Claude Product Page (Anthropic)

**URL:** claude.com/product/overview (redirected from anthropic.com/claude)
**Hero:** "Meet your thinking partner"
**Sub:** "Tackle any big, bold, bewildering challenge with Claude."

**Section structure:**
1. Hero with prompt interface
2. Capability clusters (Write / Learn / Code) with interactive prompts
3. Model tier navigation (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku)
4. Solution categories (AI agents, coding, customer support, education, financial services, healthcare)

**Word density:** Hero is 4 words + 9 words sub = 13 words total. The page is interaction-first -- the prompt interface IS the content.

**Voice:** Second-person, conversational. "Your thinking partner," "Tackle any..." Alliterative ("big, bold, bewildering"). Warm, accessible. This is consumer AI positioning, not research positioning.

**Specificity:** ZERO. No benchmarks. No performance metrics. No comparative statistics. The positioning relies entirely on capability demonstration through the interactive prompt interface. "Show, don't tell" taken to its logical conclusion.

**Credibility for researchers:** LOW as a research page (it's not trying to be one). But the "show don't tell" approach is relevant: the best proof of a system is the system itself.

**Best pattern to steal:** "Meet your thinking partner" -- 4 words, immediately humanizes the product. For Discur, the analog would be: can you anthropomorphize or personify the octonionic trie? Probably not appropriate given the esoteric/mathematical tone. But the economy of 4 words communicating a complete mental model is worth studying.

**Worst pattern to avoid:** "Big, bold, bewildering" is consumer alliteration that would be disastrous for a deep tech research page. This kind of playful copy signals "we're friendly and approachable" -- the opposite of the confident mathematical authority Discur needs.

## Implications for Discur

### The Discur Challenge

Discur occupies an unusual position in this landscape: it's a research company making a mathematical claim (octonionic trie as self-organizing memory) that is:
- Unknown (unlike AlphaFold, which carries its own weight)
- Non-obvious (octonions are niche even within mathematics)
- Verifiable (the algebra is real and checkable)
- Visual (the trie has geometric structure that can be shown)

This means Discur cannot rely on:
- Brand equity (DeepMind pattern)
- Known project names (DeepMind pattern)
- Working product demos (Linear, Claude pattern)
- Ecosystem metrics (Hugging Face pattern)
- Customer testimonials (Stripe, Vercel pattern)

Discur CAN rely on:
- Mathematical specificity (Wolfram pattern)
- The algebra itself as proof (show the equations)
- Structural diagrams as proof (show the trie)
- Team credentials (Basis pattern)
- Named, verifiable outputs (Basis/Pyro pattern)

### Recommended Copy Patterns

**1. Product-as-subject voice (Wolfram pattern)**

Not "We built a self-organizing memory system" but "The octonionic trie organizes memory across eight dimensions." Make the mathematical object the grammatical subject. Let it have properties and behaviors. This voice is rare and distinctive -- only Wolfram uses it among the companies analyzed.

**2. Quantified claims with no hedging (Wolfram + Stripe pattern)**

If the trie has properties, state them with numbers. "Eight dimensions of associative structure." "Seven lines of the Fano plane." "480 automorphisms of the octonion algebra." Precise numbers signal that you're talking about something real, not marketing.

**3. Gradient depth (Anthropic pattern)**

- Layer 1 (hero): One sentence naming the thing and its key property
- Layer 2 (first scroll): The structural claim with a diagram
- Layer 3 (deeper): The equations
- Layer 4 (link-out): The paper

**4. Named outputs as proof (Basis/HF pattern)**

Name the trie. Name the algebra. Name the specific mathematical properties being exploited. Use proper nouns for technical components. "The Fano plane" is more credible than "a geometric structure."

**5. Selective brevity (Mistral + Sakana influence)**

The hero should be 5-10 words. The entire above-fold content should be under 30 words. Density and restraint signal confidence.

### Copy Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**1. Vague aspiration (OpenAI, Cohere, Basis homepage)**
- "Solve the world's hardest problems" -- which problems?
- "Your next breakthrough" -- meaningless without context
- "A new kind of research organization" -- every org says this

**2. Enterprise SaaS template (Cohere)**
- Logo walls, vertical industry cards, "request a demo"
- Benefit-level claims without mechanism-level proof
- Second-person sales voice ("your breakthrough, powered by AI")

**3. Grandiose unfalsifiable claims (OpenAI)**
- "Will eventually lead to AGI"
- Any claim that cannot be evaluated from the page itself

**4. Consumer alliteration and playfulness (Claude product)**
- "Big, bold, bewildering"
- "Thinking partner"
- This voice signals accessibility, not authority

**5. Empty brevity (Sakana homepage)**
- Being brief is good. Being brief AND empty is not.
- Sakana's homepage is 30 words that communicate nothing technical.
- Discur's brevity should be DENSE, not hollow.

### Proposed Hero Direction

Based on this analysis, the Discur hero should:

1. Be 5-10 words
2. Name the actual thing (octonionic trie)
3. State its key property in the name or immediately below
4. Use product-as-subject voice
5. Follow with a 15-20 word sub that names the mathematical foundation

Example patterns (not final copy -- directional only):

- **Wolfram-style category claim:** "The octonionic trie: self-organizing memory from algebraic first principles"
- **Mistral-style fragment:** "Memory that organizes itself. Built on octonion algebra."
- **Stripe-style outcome:** "Eight dimensions of associative structure. One data structure."
- **Anthropic-style restraint:** "An algebraic approach to machine memory"

The strongest pattern for Discur's positioning is probably the Wolfram model: category-defining headline, product-as-subject voice, heavy quantification, no hedging, long-form depth that rewards the reader who scrolls.

### Section Structure Recommendation

Based on the analysis, research-credible landing pages for Discur should follow this approximate structure:

1. **Hero** (5-10 words + 15-20 word sub) -- name the thing, state the claim
2. **The Structure** (~150 words) -- visual diagram + dense explanation of the trie
3. **The Algebra** (~100 words + equations) -- show the mathematical foundation
4. **The Properties** (~100 words) -- quantified claims about what the structure provides
5. **The Team** (~100 words) -- credentials, institutional affiliations, named outputs
6. **The Paper** (CTA) -- link to technical paper or preprint

Total: ~500-600 words of copy. Wolfram's Mathematica page is ~1,450 words. Most AI companies are 200-400 words on their landing page. 500-600 words is appropriate for a page that needs to TEACH a concept, not just name a product.
